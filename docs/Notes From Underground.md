# Critique of Utopianism

The underground man presents the idea that total economic security will not create a utopia in the way you might think.  There is a non-rational part of human psychology that will destroy its own utopia just to "prove that he is his own individual".  You can give people the means to "sleep, eat cakes, and busy himself with the continuation of his species," and they will destroy there own prosperity.

I think this is tied to some other things I've heard and agree with, like how having things isn't fun, getting things is fun (Jimmy Carr?).  There is an element of striving needed to have a sane human mind.  Dostoevsky called this non-rational.  Rationally, humans should achieve a point of advancement technologically where we no longer need to work, we have everything we could ever need and want to survive, and rationally we should just stop, be content, and just exist.  But there is this non-rational part of our psychology that requires difficulty and striving.  I think you see this in how in many places around the world, for many people, we have no need in our daily lives for things like difficult physical activity, but we intentionally introduce this back into our lives just to have something difficult to do.

The underground man gets this inheritance, retires, spends his days doing nothing productive just living off his wealth, and is miserable and spiteful.
There is a lot of emphasis on his lack of social connections.  I think this is probably a good insight into the fact that, put side by side, meaningful social connections with people is a FAR better marker for a meaningful fulfilled life then material possessions or even economic security.
That feels like an obvious fact, but it's easy to focus so much of your time and attention in life on making money and materialistic things, when these things will never fulfillment to your life like having a happy family and group of friends.

This is a critique of things like socialism as well.  It's a faulty assumption to think that people can be put in a system where all there needs are met easily and think they will not tear down or exploit the system.  Rationally, they should, but the human mind is irrational in this way.  I think it probably is "rational" in the sense of it serving an evolutionary purpose because always needing something to stress and deal with will make you better evolutionary.  It's just in our modern society this trait ingrained in our psychology has become irrational in relation to the modern world.

Human beings don't need to be like a fed lion sleeping in a zoo.  It's like how [[Jordan Peterson]] says responsibility is what gives your life meaning.  Having complete economic security is not what makes a meaningful life.
